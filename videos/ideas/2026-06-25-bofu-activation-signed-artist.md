# BOFU Activation Ideas: "You're On CRWN — Here's The Move"

Generated 2026-06-25. **Separate bottom-of-funnel track, NOT a merge into the 90-day awareness calendar.**

These convert/activate an artist who is already on CRWN (or seriously considering it). Where the TOFU library answers *"should I own my fans?"*, every idea here answers *"I did — what do I do Monday morning, and what does it pay?"* Each is anchored to a real hip-hop/R&B artist's analog move, then translated into the concrete CRWN action + payoff. Capability shown after each (→) so you can see the feature it sells. Tagged `[S]` short / `[Y]` long.

Placement note: run this as its own series, not in the 3/day awareness rotation. Best uses: pinned/highlights on the page, the welcome DM/email to a newly signed artist, and retargeting anyone who watched the TOFU "why" content but hasn't acted.

## Stage 1 — Stand up your page before you drop

1. [S] Kendrick dropped GNX with no rollout and still hit #1. The move: have your CRWN page and smart link live BEFORE the next song, so day one has somewhere to land instead of vanishing into the algorithm. → smart links / owned page
2. [S] Frank Ocean made fans camp on a page he owned before a song even existed. The move: put your CRWN page up now and give fans one reason a week to check it, so the drop isn't your first contact. → owned page
3. [Y] Your first 7 days on CRWN, step by step. How Frank Ocean, Nipsey, and Russ each turned an owned page into income, rebuilt as a checklist you run this week. → owned page + smart links

## Stage 2 — Capture the direct line before you need it

4. [S] Bad Bunny has 114 million monthly listeners and can't email one of them. The move: switch on email and text capture so every new CRWN fan becomes a contact you own, not a number Spotify keeps. → email + SMS capture
5. [S] Beyonce's Verified Fan made people hand over their info just to prove they were real. The move: gate one exclusive behind a free signup so casual listeners turn into a list you can reach on demand. → fan capture
6. [S] Mike Jones put his phone number in his songs and still gets calls 20 years later. The move: collect fan numbers on CRWN and text your next drop straight to their phone. → SMS

## Stage 3 — Price it: free plus paid tiers, never flat

7. [S] Tech N9ne's fans call themselves Technicians and pay to belong, with no radio hit. The move: make your paid tier a title fans wear, not just a paywall. → tiers
8. [S] Stop pricing flat at one number. Build a free tier for reach, a mid tier for the regulars, and one high tier for the diehards, the way Nipsey stacked Crenshaw access. → tiers
9. [Y] How to set your CRWN tiers without guessing. Free vs paid vs high-ticket, what goes in each, priced off real fanbases (Tech N9ne, Nipsey) instead of a flat $10. → tiers

## Stage 4 — Get to your first $1,000

12. [S] 2 Chainz put a $100,000 price on one verse. The move: price a premium one-off for your top fans (a personalized verse, a feature) instead of a flat merch price. → one-time / experience

## Stage 5 — Give paying fans first

14. [S] Kennedy Ryon sends new songs to her paid Fam Club a full week before streaming. The move: release in a waterfall. Highest tier hears it first, then your lower tiers, then the free tier, then DSPs last. Your biggest fans buy first, Spotify gets the leftovers. → tiers / access windowing
15. [S] Gunna fans chase leaked snippets that wasn't even out yet. The move: feed that hunger on purpose. Post snippets and early cuts to your paid tier so the chase pays you. → tiers / early access

## Stage 6 — Onboard the new fan

17. [S] A fan who finds you today is warm for about a week, then gone. The move: auto-send new CRWN followers a welcome, a free unlock, and one ask, before the window closes. → sequences / capture

## Stage 7 — Keep them (retention beats a new follow)

18. [S] An Erykah Badu fan from 1997 is worth far more than a one-time buyer. The move: track lifetime value on CRWN and protect the long fan instead of chasing a new follower. → analytics

## Stage 11 — The two CRWN mechanics (drop order + clipper ramp)

The release-waterfall mechanic
27. [Y] The CRWN release order, highest tier first and DSPs last. Walk the full waterfall on a real rollout (Nipsey's tiered Crenshaw access, Kennedy Ryon's week-early Fam Club) so an artist sees exactly when each layer gets the song and why streaming is the final stop, not the first. → tiers / access windowing
28. [S] Most artists drop everywhere at once, so the superfan pays the same as the stranger. The move on CRWN: highest tier first, then lower tiers, then free, then DSPs, so the people who pay the most hear it first. → access windowing

The clipper share-to-earn ramp
29. [S] Playboi Carti's whole look spread through fan edit and clip accounts that got paid nothing. The move on CRWN: run share-to-earn at a high cut, ideally 100% to the clipper at the start, then step it down in increments to your standard. Pay the clippers Carti's edits never got paid. → share-to-earn
30. [S] Why front-loading the clipper cut works. Open your share-to-earn campaign at 100%, then drop it a set amount on a schedule until it hits standard. The big early bag floods the zone with clippers; the step-down protects your margin once momentum carries itself. → share-to-earn

Real proof of the waterfall (Joe Budden)
31. [Y] Joe Budden makes a reported $20 million a year putting podcast episodes behind a Patreon paywall first and sending them to YouTube free later. Break down his exact windowing as the blueprint for releasing music on CRWN: paid tier hears it first, free and DSPs last. → tiers / access windowing
32. [S] Joe Budden's roughly 70,000 Patreon subscribers, on tiers from $5 to $50, get episodes before YouTube, and the subs alone pull more than $12 million a year. The move: run your music the same way on CRWN. The fans who pay hear it first, the public catches up later. → tiers / access windowing
