## POINT 1 — WHAT A $400M LABEL DEAL ACTUALLY PAYS DRAKE FOR ONE ALBUM

Friday, May 15, 2026. Drake drops Iceman.

And it drops under OVO Sound through Republic Records.

Which means UMG.

Back in 2022, Drake re-signed with UMG. Reported $400M to $500M.

Multi-rights. Recordings. Publishing. Merch. Visual media. The full 360.

But here's what most people don't know about a $400M deal.

$400M is not a check.

It's an advance.

An advance is a loan.

UMG hands Drake the bag up front. Every dollar his music makes from that point on goes back to UMG until the $400M is paid off.

That's called recoupment.

And here's where it gets interesting.

Industry insiders say Drake already recouped the whole $400M. In under 2 years. Between his publishing, his streaming, his merch, his world tour. UMG hasn't confirmed it. Drake hasn't either.

But if it's true, Iceman is the first album in years where the money might actually hit his account.

Sounds like a win.

Wait til you see how small a win it is.

Let's run Iceman.

First week. 400K album equivalent units. UMG put the machine behind it. Radio. Spotify. Apple Music. The Toronto stunt. The Marvel collab.

400K units is about 500M streams.

Streaming pays about a third of a penny per stream.

That's $2M in revenue from week one.

Drake's superstar royalty rate is probably around 25%.

That's $500K.

$500K is a lot of money. Unless you are Drake.

If the recoupment rumors are true, that $500K actually hits his account this time. The first time in years.

$500K. From the biggest rap album of 2026. From a $400M deal.

That's the math even when everything goes right.

Now here's the trade.

UMG paid for Iceman. The whole thing.

Recording. About $2M. Marketing rollout. About $3M. Music videos. About $1M. Radio promo, press team, PR. Another $1M.

That's $7M in costs to get this album made and launched. Drake didn't write a single check.

He got the machine for free.

He just doesn't own anything that that machine made.

And here is what recoupment dont fix.

UMG still owns the masters. The deal is a licensing agreement. They hold rights to commercially exploit his music for a set period of time.

Drake doesn't get the masters back when the advance is recouped. He gets em back when the LICENSE TERM ends. That's a whole different clock.

UMG controls the sync deals. The streaming agreements. The catalog.

In January 2025, Drake filed a lawsuit against UMG. He said they boosted Kendrick Lamar's Not Like Us with bots. Pay-for-play. He said they discounted streaming rates to Spotify. He says the label that owns him helped his rival beat him in their own beef.

UMG denied all of it. They called the lawsuit illogical.

That lawsuit is still active right now as Iceman drops. The biggest album of the year is launching from inside a legal war between the artist and the label.

Drake had to sue the label that owns his music for promoting another artist on the label.

Imagine working at a job where ya manager promotes ya rival, and you can't quit because ya manager owns the work you already did.

That's the deal Iceman drops under.

Drake gets the machine, well possibly the machine. UMG gets the masters. Drake gets paid in advance. UMG gets paid forever.

And Iceman is rumored to fulfill his last album obligation under this UMG deal.

He's been telling you for years about the label being the problem.

Most fans heard it and just breezed on by it like he never said it.

But what route might he go besides a traditional record deal with somebody like a UMG.

This brings us to the next scenario, a distribution deal.

## POINT 2 — WHY OWNING YOUR MASTERS CAN PUT YOU $4M IN THE HOLE

So Drake's lawyers are at the table. UMG wants a new deal.

Drake's looking at the door.

What if he just walks?

There's a thing called a distribution deal. It's highly speculated that he may go the route of a distribution deal with someone like Sony.

The pitch is simple.

You pay for everything. You own everything. Sony just puts it on the platforms for him and takes a small cut.

The math look like Drake is winning.

Sony takes 15-20%. Drake keeps 80-85%.

It's the same Iceman and around the same first week numbers. 400K first week. $2M in revenue.

Distributor takes $400K. Drake keeps $1.6M.

Compare that to the $500K he was getting on UMG. He just tripled his cut.

He owns the masters. The catalog pays him 80% forever.

This sounds like the answer.

But is it?

The label machine is not just a cut. It is a machine.

UMG had radio relationships built over 50 years. Editorial playlists at every DSP. Press connections. Retail partners. The Marvel collab. The Toronto ice sculpture. The Iceman Episodes livestreams.

Who built all that?

UMG's marketing team. UMG's promo team. UMG's PR firm. UMG's radio guys with 30 year relationships at every station in America.

Distribution doesn't come with that.

Distribution comes with an upload button.

Without the machine, Drake doesn't do 400K first week.

Maybe he does 300K.

Maybe less.

Now here comes the bill.

Drake has to pay for Iceman himself.

Recording features and production could be  $2M. Marketing could be around $3M. Videos? $1M. An indpenedent radio team, PR firm, marketing consultants. Another $1M.

That's $5-6M out of his own pocket. Before the album drops.

Let's say he does ~300K first week at distribution rates. That's $1.5M in revenue.

Distributor takes 20%. He keeps $1.2M.

He spent $5M plus, but he only made ~$1.2M.

Drake just lost $4M on his own album week one.

The biggest artist in the world. Negative $4M.


Imagine Drake, who's the biggest artist in the game right now, going home that night.

He look at his accountant and like "Look, I then made $4M less than nothing on the album that took me 2 YEARS to make."

And his accountant is like, "Yeah but you own it though."

"Own what? You talkin bout an album nobody can hear because I couldn't afford to put it on the radio and get it higher on the charts?"

This is the thing nobody tells you about owning ya masters.

It's worth nothing if you can't get it in front of people.

A master of a song nobody plays is just a hard drive in ya closet.

Now over time the math flips.

Streams keep paying 80%. Year 2. Year 3. Year 5.

The catalog earns it back. He owns it. It compounds. By year 5, he's is in the green.

But the first 18 months he's bleeding.

Most artists who try this quit before the math even turns.

And this is the rug pull for an artist who's NOT Drake.

YOUT don't have $5M to spend on rolling out an album. You have $5K.

The distribution deal looks better than a label until you realize it costs the same. The label just spends it for you and takes the masters as payment.

So is the answer the label? Or the distributor?

Or is the answer dropping on a platform where he keeps?

Right now UMG owns Drake. Distribution would cost too much.

So can Drake launch from a platform where he has more ownership without losing everything? If so how?
