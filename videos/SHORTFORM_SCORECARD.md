# Short-Form Scorecard

Grade every short-form video on this **before posting**. Built from the first 12 published videos (1.9k–117k views). Validated: it correctly ranks #2 and #21 (the 117k/60k breakouts) at the top and #23/#10 (under 2.5k) at the bottom.

## The model: two engines

Views are an output. You only control two inputs:

- **Skip rate = the GATE.** Decides if the algorithm tests you wider. Every breakout in the data is under 36% skip. Nothing over 38% broke 16k unless shares rescued it.
- **Share rate = the MULTIPLIER.** Amplifies whatever the gate lets through. Two videos at 8.7% share landed at 2.6k and 15.6k — the difference was their skip rate.

**Breakout = skip under 36% AND share at or above 3%.** Only #2 and #21 hit both.

## The curiosity gap (the actual test — read this first)

A real curiosity gap is a **question, not a withheld number.** The hook plants ONE specific question in the viewer's head, that exact question stays unanswered through the middle, and the **final line is the answer to it.** Question and answer are a matched pair.

**The question must be in the viewer's head by the END of the first spoken sentence.** Sentence 1 either IS the question, or it's a loaded statement that forces the question on its own. A setup sentence followed by the question in sentence 2 or 3 is too slow — the scroll is already lost. Best practice: make sentence 1 the literal question, still naming the artist + a concrete noun (e.g. *"How much of Jay-Z's billion came from actually rapping?"*, *"What was a plain G-Unit tank top actually worth?"*).

**The test:** state, in one plain sentence, the question the hook plants — and confirm it's planted by the end of sentence 1. Then confirm (a) the FINAL line delivers the specific payoff that answers it, and (b) the answer is NOT given in sentence 2 and the specific payoff is not stated anywhere before the final line. The middle SHOULD build context, raise the stakes, even re-pose the question — that is exactly what holds the viewer watching. It just must not spill the specific payoff. **Building context mid-script is fine and necessary; only the payoff is withheld** (do not confuse "context appears mid-script" with "the answer appears mid-script" — they are different, and only the second one fails). **Hold the answer as long as possible: the longer the question hangs unanswered, the lower the skip rate, and lowest skip is the whole goal.** If you can't name the question, there is no gap.

It fails when:
- The hook is a statement, not a question-sparker, so the viewer asks nothing. *e.g. #29 "Carti's look spread free and clippers got nothing" — there is no question you're dying to have answered.*
- The answer arrives in sentence 2 (instant resolve = no gap = high skip).
- The specific PAYOFF is spilled before the final line. (Context, stakes, and re-posing the question mid-script are FINE — only the payoff itself is withheld.)
- The ending answers a DIFFERENT question than the hook posed (mismatch).
- A number is withheld but it doesn't answer any question the viewer actually cared about.

It works when the hook makes the viewer NEED one specific answer and the script makes them wait: *"What happens when you sign a $2M deal?"* → held → *"it was a loan, the music had to earn $11M before he saw a dime."* The question is explicit; the last line answers it.

## Score it: 100 points

### HOOK — feeds the gate (30 pts)
- [ ] **Plants a specific question by the END of sentence 1; answers it ONLY in the last line** (10) — *the FIRST sentence puts ONE clear question in the viewer's head (best: sentence 1 IS the question). A withheld number is not enough; there must be a question the number will answer, and it must land in sentence 1, not 2 or 3. See "The curiosity gap" above. #2 asked "what happens when you sign $2M?" in line 1 → 117k. #9 answered it in line 1 → 15.6k.*
- [ ] **A named artist appears in the FIRST sentence** (10) — *every sub-36% hook opens on a person. #23 put Megan in beat 2 → died. Abstract "This is how you…/If you…" openers with no name are the #1 skip-rate failure.*
- [ ] **No math / no numbers as the opening line** (5) — *"yall ready for this math" and "if 1 fan pays..." had the two worst skip rates.*
- [ ] **Concrete dramatic noun** — a deal, a lawsuit, a $ amount, "masters" (5) — *not an abstract process like "cashed checks mid-lawsuit" (#10 → 2.1k).*

> **The two-axis balance (critical).** Low skip wants the artist + a concrete noun up front; the held gap wants the payoff withheld to the end. They conflict ONLY if the concrete noun you front is the *outcome*. Resolve it by fronting the **setup, not the result**: name the artist + what they *did* ("Dr. Dre put his name on a pair of headphones"), and hold what it *earned* ("…paid him a reported $600M") for the final line. Stating the result in sentence one satisfies skip but tanks the gap — front the setup, hold the number.

### SHAREABLE PAYOFF — feeds the multiplier (25 pts)

> **The novelty test (the share engine — this is what actually caps reach).** Skip is the GATE; share is the MULTIPLIER, and share is driven by SURPRISE, not by a withheld number. Ask one question: does the final line make the viewer think *"wait, WHAT?"* or *"I knew that"*? A payoff that **CONFIRMS** a belief the audience already holds — *"own your masters," "streaming pays pennies," "fans beat streams," "the label takes the bigger cut"* — does NOT get shared, even with a perfect curiosity gap. The payoff must **REVERSE** a belief or drop a number they didn't see coming. **Real data:** #8 (ice block) and #11 (100M = spare change) both held the gap fine but landed on "he owns his masters" → 0.4–0.6% share → capped at ~3k. #2 (the $2M deal was actually a loan he owed $11M on) reversed a belief → 3.4% share → 117k. **If the answer confirms what they already believe, re-point it to something that surprises.** Banned as the payoff because they're belief-confirming AND saturated: "own your masters," "streaming pays pennies / fans pay more," "the label takes more," "streams build the label / fans build the artist."

- [ ] **The final line ANSWERS the hook's question with a SURPRISE** (10) — *reverses a belief or drops an unexpected number; screenshot-worthy. NOT a familiar restate. #2's "200K fans = $24M/yr" answers "what happens when you sign $2M?" and flips "a deal is winning" into "a deal is a debt." See the novelty test above.*
- [ ] **A clean "they don't tell you" reveal/betrayal lands** (8)
- [ ] **Payoff scales down to the viewer** (your 50 fans = $6k) (7)

### TOPIC + FIGURE (20 pts)
- [ ] **Household name** (Drake / Kendrick / Jay / Wayne tier) (8)
- [ ] **Specific event or number, not a generic concept** (7) — *#9's pure concept needed shares to survive.*
- [ ] **Fresh tension, not stale news** (5) — *"Kendrick won 5 Grammys" as a hook = stale → 45.2% skip.*

### IMAGE (15 pts)
- [ ] **Recognizable sharpie PORTRAIT of the named artist** (8) — *#21 is the only video with real face portraits and it's #2 overall. Diagram-only Drake videos mostly sat at 2–6k.*
- [ ] **One clear focal visual, not a wall of text** (4)
- [ ] **Pure white flat sheet, 100% handwriting** (3)

### CAROUSEL PRE-SEED — insurance, not a ceiling (10 pts)
- [ ] **Ran a carousel on the same topic days prior that performed** (10) — *rescues a weak hook (#15: 44.9% skip still hit 10.6k). Not required if the hook is strong (#2 hit 117k with no carousel).*

|---|---|---|

## The one rule if you remember nothing else

**Plant a question. Hold its answer for the end. Put a face on the page.**
A gap is a question the viewer needs answered, not just a number you hid. If you can't say the question in one sentence, there's no gap. Front-loading the answer trades ~8x your reach for a few extra shares.
